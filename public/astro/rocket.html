01 How to Use This Sandbox

Overall flow

Pick a planet, tweak the rocket sliders, click ▲ LAUNCH, watch dynamic pressure peak as the rocket climbs. Click ↻ RESET to retry with different settings.

Planet selector

Earth is the baseline. Mars is rocket paradise (thin air, weak gravity). Venus is a nightmare (thick CO₂, Earth-like gravity). Moon has no atmosphere at all. Titan has weak gravity but 4× Earth surface density. Each preset switches gravity and atmosphere model.

The five sliders

Thrust is engine push (kN). Fuel mass is how much propellant you carry — more fuel means longer burn but heavier rocket. Dry mass is the empty vehicle. Cd (~0.5 typical) is shape factor; lower = more streamlined. A is the frontal cross-section facing the airstream.

Telemetry tiles

T+ is time since launch. The middle block shows altitude, velocity, and acceleration in real time. Q (dynamic pressure) is the headline number — the aerodynamic stress on the airframe. Drag is the resulting force. Fuel ticks down to zero. MAX-Q (PEAK) records the worst moment of stress and the altitude where it happened — and stays after burnout so you can read it.

The two charts

Altitude vs Time shows the climb profile. Dynamic Pressure vs Time shows the Q curve — rises as speed builds in dense air, peaks somewhere mid-flight, falls as the air thins. Together they tell you when and where the rocket suffered most.

Why Q and Max-Q matter

Q = ½ρv² is the aerodynamic stress per area. Max-Q is the worst moment of that stress. Real rockets throttle their engines down at Max-Q so the airframe doesn't snap. This sim runs constant thrust (no throttle), which makes Max-Q artificially severe — that's deliberate, to make the danger viscerally obvious.

Visual cues

The pad and terrain are scale cues. The open arcs around the rocket appear only when Q is meaningful; they show aerodynamic loading, not a force field. Low-altitude exhaust may flare against the pad, but landing guidance is not modeled.

02 Dynamic Pressure Drives Drag

Your friend asked about compression ratio and friction ratio. Here's the connection: in rocket flight, both come out of the same airflow term, ½ρv².

Density (ρ) times velocity squared, halved. Call it dynamic pressure (q) when you're thinking of it as a squeezing force per unit area on the airframe — that's the "compression" feel. Multiply it by drag coefficient and cross-sectional area and now it's drag force — the friction slowing you down. They're not literally the same thing (drag also depends on shape, flow regime, Mach number, Reynolds number), but they share that ½ρv² heart.

This is why thick atmospheres (Venus) and high speeds (anywhere) both punish a rocket: each one pumps up that ½ρv² term. Toggle Educational mode to see the equation strip live.

03 Max-Q: Peak Aerodynamic Stress

A rocket starts in dense atmosphere but moves slowly — small q. As it climbs, density drops fast (exponentially), but velocity grows even faster. Q spikes, peaks somewhere in the middle, then collapses as the rocket leaves atmosphere behind. That peak is Max-Q.

Many orbital launches hit Max-Q in the lower stratosphere — often roughly 10–15 km — though it varies by vehicle and trajectory. Falcon 9 sees about 35 kPa; Saturn V was about 33 kPa. Engineers design the airframe to survive that worst moment, and during a launch broadcast, "passing through Max-Q" is the call you hear when everyone exhales.

Note on this sim: real rockets actively throttle down as they approach Max-Q (BE-3 can throttle to about 18% of full thrust), then throttle back up once the air thins. This sim runs constant thrust until fuel-out, which makes Max-Q artificially severe compared to a real well-flown ascent. That's a feature, not a bug — it makes the danger of high q viscerally obvious. Just don't read the absolute numbers as predictions.

04 A Tour of Five Worlds

EARTH — the baseline

The closest real-world inspiration for this sandbox. Surface gravity 9.81 m/s², thick nitrogen-oxygen atmosphere, scale height ~8.5 km. Max-Q is non-trivial but manageable; a healthy thrust-to-weight ratio gets you above 100 km (the Kármán convention, edge of space) with a suborbital-scale propellant load.

MARS — rocket paradise

Gravity is ~38% of Earth's and the atmosphere is less than 1% as dense. Dynamic pressure barely registers. The same rocket that struggles on Earth would coast up effortlessly here — which is why Mars Sample Return's ascent vehicle is tiny compared to anything launched from Earth.

VENUS — rocket nightmare

Gravity is similar to Earth's, but surface pressure is about 92× Earth's, and surface density is about 53× Earth sea-level air (65 vs 1.225 kg/m³). Pressure-wise, it's like being nearly a kilometer underwater — though the fluid is still hot CO₂ gas, not liquid water. Try the sim with default rocket settings: Max-Q goes absurd, and the rocket may not even break free of the lower atmosphere.

Reality check: this sim simulates the same mathematical rocket on Venus — it does not model the fact that an Earth-class engine wouldn't actually function at 92 bar ambient pressure (the nozzle physics break down) or survive the 460°C surface heat. Venus mode shows what happens to the equations when atmosphere goes extreme, not what would happen to a buildable vehicle.

MOON — pure thrust vs gravity

No atmosphere, no drag, no Max-Q. Q stays at zero the whole flight. This is the cleanest possible test of "thrust vs gravity" because there's no air pushing back at all. The Apollo Lunar Module ascent stage worked because of this — minimal engine, minimal fuel, just escape the gravity well.

TITAN — the strangest case

Saturn's largest moon. Gravity is only 14% of Earth's, but the atmosphere is ~4× denser than Earth's at the surface (5.3 vs 1.225 kg/m³). This combination is so extreme that humans could plausibly fly with wing-like aids. A rocket here faces moderate Q from the dense low air but barely fights gravity at all.

Atmosphere caveat: the 21 km scale height used here describes Titan's lower atmosphere, where drag during ascent matters most. Titan's real upper atmosphere is far more extended than this simple exponential model suggests — Cassini measured drag at altitudes up to ~975 km. The 600 km "target" line is a high-atmosphere reference marker, not a Kármán line.

05 Glossary & Formulas

Quick reference for every variable and equation the simulator uses. Units in SI throughout.

Variables

h — altitude above surface (m)

v — vertical velocity (m/s); positive = up

a — acceleration (m/s²)

m — total rocket mass at this instant (kg) = dry mass + remaining fuel

T — thrust force (N) — set by the slider, constant while fuel > 0

g₀ — surface gravity of the selected planet (m/s²)

g(h) — gravity at altitude h (m/s²)

R — planet radius (m)

ρ₀ — atmospheric density at the surface (kg/m³)

ρ(h) — atmospheric density at altitude h (kg/m³)

H — atmospheric scale height (m); altitude over which density drops by factor of e (~63%)

Cd — drag coefficient (dimensionless); ~0.5 for a typical rocket shape

A — cross-sectional area facing the airstream (m²)

q — dynamic pressure (Pa = N/m²)

F_d — drag force (N)

F_net — net force on the rocket (N)

Isp — specific impulse (s); fuel efficiency. Higher Isp = more thrust per kg of fuel burned

Core equations

Dynamic pressure  q = ½ · ρ(h) · v²  [Pa]

Drag force (signed)  F_drag = −½ · ρ(h) · Cd · A · v · |v|  [N]  — automatically opposes motion regardless of direction (the telemetry display shows |F_drag|, the magnitude)

Atmospheric density  ρ(h) = ρ₀ · e^(−h/H)  [kg/m³]  — exponential isothermal model

Gravity at altitude  g(h) = g₀ · (R / (R+h))²  [m/s²]  — inverse-square law

Net force (vertical)  F_net = T + F_drag − m · g(h)  [N]

Acceleration  a = F_net / m  [m/s²]

Fuel mass flow rate  ṁ = T / (Isp · g_Earth)  [kg/s]  — note this uses Earth's g₀ (9.81) by convention even on other planets, because Isp is defined that way

Mass update  m(t+dt) = m(t) − ṁ · dt  (while fuel remains; partial-step on the burnout boundary)

Numerical integration

The sim uses semi-implicit Euler (also known as Euler-Cromer) at a fixed timestep dt = 0.05 s, with ~5× real-time acceleration. Each frame: compute forces at current state → update velocity → use the new velocity to update altitude → update fuel → repeat. Updating position with the new velocity (rather than the old one) makes this scheme more stable than plain forward Euler for motion problems. It does not make the system energy-conserving — thrust adds energy, drag removes it, mass changes, and impacts clamp state — but it's accurate enough for an interactive sandbox at this timestep.

When fuel runs out mid-step, the timestep is split into a burn segment and a coast segment so thrust impulse is delivered exactly, not over-applied.

Key constants used

PLANETS

Earth: g₀=9.81, R=6,371 km, ρ₀=1.225, H=8.5 km, target=100 km (Kármán convention)

Mars: g₀=3.71, R=3,389 km, ρ₀=0.020, H=11.1 km, target=80 km

Venus: g₀=8.87, R=6,052 km, ρ₀=65, H=15.9 km, target=250 km

Moon: g₀=1.62, R=1,737 km, ρ₀=0 (no atmosphere), target=50 km

Titan: g₀=1.35, R=2,575 km, ρ₀=5.3, H=21 km, target=600 km (high-atmosphere reference, not a Kármán line)

ROCKET (defaults, New Shepard-class)

Thrust: 700 kN (BE-3 ballpark; sea-level rating is ~490 kN and vacuum is ~710 kN, so 700 approximates the average effective thrust over an ascent as ambient pressure drops)

Dry mass: 20,000 kg

Fuel mass: 15,000 kg

Cd: 0.5

A: 10.8 m² (≈3.7 m diameter)

Isp: 350 s (representative atmospheric LH2/LOX value for this sandbox; Blue Origin publishes BE-3 thrust but not a clear public sea-level Isp)

06 What This Sandbox Does NOT Capture

Every model lies a little. Here are the honest limitations so your friend doesn't draw wrong conclusions:

  • 1D vertical only. Real rockets do a "gravity turn" early in flight to reach orbit, not just shoot straight up. This sim is suborbital ascent only — the New Shepard regime, not the Falcon 9 regime.
  • Constant thrust. Real engines throttle down at Max-Q. Here thrust is fixed until fuel runs out, which makes Max-Q higher than it would be on a real well-designed flight.
  • No reusable-booster guidance. Modern boosters can restart engines, deploy landing gear, and target a pad. This sandbox may draw low-altitude exhaust visually, but it does not reserve propellant, restart engines, deploy legs, or solve a soft touchdown.
  • Constant Cd. Drag coefficient actually changes with speed (especially around the speed of sound). We use a single Cd ≈ 0.5 for the whole flight.
  • No aerodynamic heating. A real rocket plowing through Venus would also be cooking from compression heating. We track only mechanical force.
  • Isothermal atmosphere. We model density with a single exponential scale height, ignoring temperature variation with altitude. Good enough to feel the difference between worlds; not good enough to design a real vehicle.
  • No orbital mechanics. We're tracking ascent, not orbit insertion. To "reach the target" here means crossing a chosen altitude marker. Only Earth's 100 km marker is the standard Kármán-line convention; the others are reasonable reference altitudes for those worlds, not hard physical boundaries.

07 Things to Try

→ Set thrust to minimum on Earth — does it even leave the pad? Now switch to Mars without changing anything.

→ Crank thrust up on Venus. Watch Max-Q. Can you find a thrust that gets to 50 km without dynamic pressure exceeding what Falcon 9 survives (~35 kPa)?

→ Compare Moon and Mars with identical settings. The atmosphere on Mars is so thin that it almost doesn't matter — but the difference shows up in the velocity curve.

→ Drop drag coefficient to 0.1 (very streamlined) and increase cross-section to simulate a wider rocket. Which has more impact on Venus?

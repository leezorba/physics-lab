01 How to Use This Lab

Overall flow

Pick a gravity environment, pick a tab, then push the relevant slider until the block moves. Telemetry tiles, status pill, and the velocity chart all respond live.

The three tabs

Sliding Block teaches the static-to-kinetic friction transition. Inclined Plane teaches the slip angle (when tan θ exceeds μ_s). Stick-Slip teaches the spring-driven cycle that makes hinges squeak and bows make violins sing.

Gravity environment

Switching Earth, Moon, Mars, or Custom recomputes normal force (F_n = m·g) and therefore the friction force. Same coefficient of friction can produce very different behavior depending on weight.

Telemetry tiles

F_n is normal force. Static max is the threshold force needed to start sliding. F_f is the friction force right now. Velocity is live; Peak v records the highest velocity reached this run and persists after the block stops.

Velocity chart

Below the canvas, plots block velocity over time for the current run. The shape tells you what friction did: a straight line is constant acceleration, a curve that flattens means kinetic friction is eating the gain. Freezes at impact so you can read the history.

Friction readings panel

Top-right card (click + to expand): paste a column of measured μ values. Shows mean, sample standard deviation, and a μ ± 2s scatter band. Use μ_min when relying on friction to hold (brakes, anchors); use μ_max when sizing motors that must overcome it.

02 Glossary

F_f

Friction force. Static friction grows only as large as needed until its maximum; kinetic friction acts during sliding.

F_n

Normal force, perpendicular to the contact surface. On a level surface in this toy model, F_n = m·g.

μ_s

Static coefficient of friction, used to decide when a resting object starts moving.

μ_k

Kinetic coefficient of friction, used after sliding begins. Usually lower than μ_s.

θ

Ramp angle for the inclined plane.

N

Number of measured coefficient readings pasted into the stats panel.

s

Sample standard deviation, computed with Bessel's correction using n - 1.

k

Spring constant in the stick-slip tab. A stiffer spring builds force faster.

03 Core Equations

F_f = μ · F_n

Coulomb friction: friction scales with coefficient and normal force.

s = sqrt(Σ(x_i - x̄)² / (n - 1))

Sample standard deviation with Bessel's correction.

μ_min = μ̄ - 2s, μ_max = μ̄ + 2s

Engineering scatter band. This is not a confidence interval for the mean.

Δμ = μ_s - μ_k

Stick-slip becomes more pronounced as the static-to-kinetic gap grows.

04 Why This Matters Off-Earth

  • Lunar and Martian dust are practical machinery problems because abrasive particles can invade seals, joints, wheels, and contact surfaces.
  • Weight-coupled friction, such as tire traction or gravity-pad anchors, scales with F_n = m·g. The same vehicle can have very different traction margin on the Moon, Mars, and Earth.
  • Mechanically clamped friction, such as a disc brake caliper or vise, is different: clamp force comes from the mechanism, not the vehicle's weight.
  • Low-pressure or clean-surface environments can increase static friction in some contacts. The toggle in this app is qualitative, not a validated Mars rover failure model — and Mars itself is not hard vacuum, with surface pressure around 0.6% of Earth's.

05 What This Does Not Capture

Coulomb friction is empirical, not fundamental. Real contact behavior depends on material pairing, temperature, surface roughness, contamination, wear, lubrication, contact geometry, loading history, and sliding speed.

The stick-slip tab uses a sudden μ_s to μ_k switch. Real transitions are smoother, and real systems can include compliance, damping, deformation, and acoustic vibration that are outside this model.

The μ ± 2s readout in the stats panel is a scatter or tolerance band — it estimates where ~95% of individual μ readings fall under a normality assumption. It is not a 95% confidence interval for the mean (that would be μ̄ ± t·s/√n and is much narrower for large n). For design conservatism you usually want the scatter band, but the distinction matters when reporting numbers.
